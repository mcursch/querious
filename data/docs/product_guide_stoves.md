## Overview

Acme Outfitters carries three stove families: **FlameCore** canister stoves for backpacking, **DuraBlaze** liquid-fuel stoves for expedition and cold-weather use, and **SolFlame** alcohol and solid-fuel systems for ultralight enthusiasts. ## Fuel Types

Understanding fuel types is essential for choosing the right stove and for safely transporting fuel.

### Canister Fuel (Isobutane / Propane Blend)

Pre-pressurized canisters containing a mixture of isobutane (typically 80%) and propane (typically 20%). Screw-thread canisters use the EN417 standard thread, which is universal across all FlameCore stoves and most major brands.

**Advantages:** Clean-burning, precise heat control, no priming required, compact.

**Limitations:** Performance degrades below approximately 20°F (−7°C) due to reduced vapor pressure; canisters cannot be air-shipped (IATA Packing Instruction 959) — customers in Alaska, Hawaii, and international destinations must source fuel locally.

**Acme canister sizes:** 100 g (weekend), 230 g (2–4 days), 450 g (week+). All are compatible with all FlameCore and any EN417 canister stove.

### White Gas (Naphtha)

Refined liquid fuel burned in vaporizing stoves. Universally available in North America and most international destinations. Burns hot even in extreme cold.

**Advantages:** Excellent cold-weather and high-altitude performance; fuel is globally available; economical for extended expeditions.

**Limitations:** Requires priming and maintenance; less convenient than canister; spills are flammable and require careful handling.

### Alcohol

Denatured alcohol (ethanol + methanol denaturant). Used in ultralight stoves.

**Advantages:** Fuel available at most hardware and grocery stores; stoves are extremely simple with no moving parts; quiet operation.

**Limitations:** Low heat output (slow boil times vs. canister or white gas); affected by wind and cold; no flame control — alcohol stoves burn until fuel is consumed.

### Solid Fuel Tablets

Hexamine (Esbit) or similar compressed fuel tablets. Used in ultralight emergency and supplemental cooking.

**Advantages:** Zero spill risk; very light; shelf-stable for years.

**Limitations:** Very low heat output; leaves a residue on pot; bitter taste can transfer to water if not used in a properly designed stove.

## FlameCore Series — Canister Stoves

### FlameCore Micro

The FlameCore Micro is an ultralight screw-top canister stove optimized for solo backpackers.

- **Weight (stove only):** 2.8 oz (80 g)
- **Fuel type:** EN417 isobutane/propane canister
- **Boil time:** 3.5 min per 1 L (72°F ambient, no wind)
- **Burn rate:** 7 g/min at max output
- **BTU output:** 7,500 BTU/hr
- **Flame control:** Continuous via valve; simmer-capable down to ~10% output
- **Pot support:** Three folding pot-support arms; max recommended pot diameter 6 in
- **Integrated igniter:** Piezo push-button
- **Wind resistance:** Low; use with windscreen (see safety notes)
- **Best for:** Solo backpackers counting grams; simple boil-and-eat meals

### FlameCore Trail

The FlameCore Trail is a full-featured backpacking stove with a more stable pot support for larger cooksets.

- **Weight (stove only):** 3.5 oz (100 g)
- **Fuel type:** EN417 isobutane/propane canister
- **Boil time:** 3.0 min per 1 L (72°F ambient, no wind)
- **Burn rate:** 8 g/min at max output
- **BTU output:** 9,200 BTU/hr
- **Flame control:** Continuous valve with marked simmer position
- **Pot support:** Four folding arms; max recommended pot diameter 8 in
- **Integrated igniter:** Piezo push-button
- **Wind resistance:** Moderate; integrated radiant burner head reduces wind impact by ~30% vs. Micro
- **Best for:** Solo to two-person backpacking; versatile cooking (not just boiling)

### FlameCore System 1200

An integrated canister cooking system where the stove and pot are purpose-matched.

- **Weight (stove + pot + lid):** 12.5 oz (354 g)
- **Pot volume:** 1.2 L hard-anodized aluminum
- **Fuel type:** EN417 canister (canister stores inside pot)
- **Boil time:** 2.5 min per 0.5 L (72°F, no wind) due to heat-exchange fin design
- **Fuel efficiency:** ~20% better than conventional canister stoves (less fuel per boil)
- **Best for:** Backpackers who want maximum efficiency and an all-in-one system

## DuraBlaze Series — Liquid-Fuel Stoves

### DuraBlaze XL

The DuraBlaze XL is Acme Outfitters' premier liquid-fuel stove for mountaineering and extended expeditions.

- **Weight (stove + fuel bottle 1 L):** 1 lb 9 oz (700 g)
- **Fuel types:** White gas (primary); can be converted to run unleaded auto gas, kerosene, or diesel with optional jet kit (sold separately)
- **Boil time:** 4.5 min per 1 L (72°F ambient, no wind); 5.5 min at −20°F
- **BTU output:** 10,500 BTU/hr
- **Cold-weather performance:** Rated to −40°F with white gas; performance drops ~15% at temperatures below −20°F
- **High-altitude performance:** Tested at 20,000 ft (6,100 m) with full performance
- **Priming method:** Pressurize fuel bottle with integrated pump; drizzle fuel into priming cup; ignite manually
- **Flame control:** Full range from low simmer to max
- **Maintenance:** Annual cleaning of jet and generator recommended; rebuild kit available
- **Best for:** Winter camping, high-altitude expeditions, international travel where canister fuel is unavailable

## Safety Notes

### Canister Stove Safety

- **Never use a windscreen that encloses the canister.** An enclosed canister will overheat and can rupture or explode. Use windscreens that shield only the pot and leave the canister fully exposed to ambient air.
- Always use stoves on a stable, level, non-flammable surface. Never use inside a tent or enclosed shelter — carbon monoxide poisoning is a serious risk even with the tent door partially open.
- Do not over-tighten canister connections; finger-tight is sufficient.
- Allow stove and canister to cool before packing.
- Dispose of empty canisters properly: puncture and recycle where accepted.

### Liquid-Fuel Stove Safety

- Prime in a ventilated area. Keep fuel away from open flame during priming.
- Never fill the fuel bottle more than 90% full; fuel expands with heat and the pump mechanism requires airspace.
- In the event of a flare-up during priming, do not panic — back away and allow the priming fuel to burn off. Keep a small fire blanket or water nearby when priming in camp.
- Store fuel bottles with the cap sealed; do not store in sealed containers (tent vestibule with sealed fly) — fumes can accumulate.

### General Safety

- Keep stoves away from flammable materials (dry grass, tent fabric, leaf litter).
- Never leave a lit stove unattended.
- Teach new users proper lighting procedure before their first solo trip.
- Check stove O-rings and seals before each trip season; replace if cracked or dry.

## Care and Maintenance

### After Each Trip

- Let the stove cool completely before packing.
- Wipe exterior with a dry cloth to remove food residue.
- For canister stoves: check the burner head for clogging; a clogged jet can be cleaned with the stove cleaning tool (included with FlameCore Trail and System 1200).

### Seasonal Maintenance

- Canister stoves: inspect pot supports for bending or cracking; replace if deformed.
- DuraBlaze XL: disassemble and clean the jet and generator at least once per season or after every 50 hours of use. Rebuild kits available from Acme Outfitters accessories.
- Alcohol stoves: inspect for corrosion or dents that could impede airflow; replace if compromised.
